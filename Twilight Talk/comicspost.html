The Twilight Zone Comics

August 9, 2018 by Douglas Hawkes

In the 60's and 70's, horror comics began a new wave coinciding with science fiction tv series', both genres struck gold. You had 'Tales of Mystery', 'Ripley's Believe it or Not', 'The Outer Limits', and in 1961, Western Publishing helped add 'The Twilight Zone' to the rapidly expanding list.

A Brief History

The Twilight Zone comic featured Rod Serling introducing original stories and sometimes adaptations of episodes all which had a life span longer than the critically acclaimed TV series and Serling himself. After about 95 issues between years 1961 and 1962, NOW Comics started publishing a spinnoff of the 80's revival series in 1991. Then skip to 2008, when Savannah college partnered with publisher Walker and Company to produce a series of graphic novel adaptations from the Serling series. Last but not least in December of 2013, another comics publisher named Dynamite Entertainment had a multi-issue series written by J. Michael Straczynski.

The Twilight Zone - 1962 - Issue 1

I have read the first issue of the 1962 comic, published by Gold Key. The first issue contains three short stories and one really small one page story with the first story being about twelve pages long. At the end of the comic book they also add in one page of some history of General Custer's last stand and how the only survivor was a wounded horse named, "Comanche", that they gave a place of honor to in all the Seventh Calvary parades. It starts out with Serling's introduction about the frozen ball of a planet Jupiter, then moves on to two characters on a snowy mountain. The story is really confusing and kind of all over the place. After one of the characters falls into a random hole in the snow, he encounters a bear that he attempts to shoot with a gun and it does absolutely nothing which really baffles me.

He escapes the bear with help from a little girl who leads them into a small room with four icy wall mirrors. From here we have the strangest dialogue between the two before the man without even thinking destroys the mirror causing himself to get trapped by tumbling ice and snow. Meanwhile his partner goes into the hole he fell in and miraculously finds him within seconds. The rescuee asks about the girl and his partner says there was no girl, so then they board a ship and leave what I'm guessing is the planet Jupiter to go back to Earth.

Issue 1 Conclusion

Maybe I'm just used to the show where I get a beter explanation of the story, because this first story was really confusing and to my knowledge had no real plot or maybe it was just incredibly bland. The illustrations were decent for the time and has that old vintage look to it makes you feel like your holding a relic. The only cool part in the first story is that Serling is illustrated in the last panel doing his outro spiel.

It's disappointing because the second story is not bad but it's like an adaptation of "The 7th is Made Up of Phantoms", except the main character is running from the law when he stumbles into an open window leading to Custer's last stand exhibit which he touches and is instantly teleported to the past. The last panel's illustration of the battle of little big horn's exhibit is actually quite vivid and exceptional.

The third story is suprisingly good and gives you that old classic Twilight Zone feel of eerieness. I can't say this story reminds me of any other classic episodes but if you all can name one please do so in the comments please. This story keeps you interested and has everything a Rod Serling piece of work would have stuffed in a comic book. At the end of the comic book they also add in one page of some history of General Custer's last stand and how the only survivor was a wounded horse named, "Comanche", that they gave a place of honor to in all the Seventh Calvary parades.

Thank you Twilight Zone fans for coming to my page and discussing a one of a kind anthology tv series. Please show support in the comments and let me know what other posts you guys would want to see, thank you.
